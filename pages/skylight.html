The bet · my first 90 days, on paper

The Family OS grows up, not just down

Buddy added a member at the youngest end of the family. The symmetric move — the one hiding in plain sight — is the oldest end: aging parents working to keep their independence, and the adult kids quietly carrying the worry.

The next member of the family OS

A senior surface — software-first, built on the radical simplicity Skylight already leads on. The parent keeps their independence; the family keeps their peace of mind.

The insight

Skylight's origin product, the Frame, is already in grandparents' homes nationwide — adult kids buy it as the simplest possible way to stay connected. That's a warm beachhead the OS under-uses. The young end got Buddy. The old end is wide open.

The opportunity

Grow the OS up — software-led, riding the Frame and Calendar hardware already in the home. Same buyer (the sandwich-generation adult already running the family calendar), and two users whose interests finally align instead of pulling apart.

The minimum lovable product
  • A giant-type, high-contrast day-at-a-glance: today's plan, appointments, gentle medication and hydration nudges.
  • One-tap "call my daughter" and a stream of family photos — isolation is the real health risk.
  • A dignity-preserving loop: the parent taps "I'm okay today"; family adds a note or appointment without it feeling like surveillance.
How we'd know it's working

Attach rate into existing Frame-owning households (the warm funnel), senior daily engagement, and the real prize — fewer "is Mom okay?" coordination calls. The mental-load promise, aimed one generation up.

Other bets I weighed, and why this won. I looked at deepening "what's for dinner" (the strongest AI flex, but it reads as feature-deepening more than a next major step) and a broader household-operations layer (powerful, but it drifts toward a generic to-do app). Growing up wins first: it's a warm beachhead rather than a cold start, it points Skylight's simplicity superpower at the audience that rewards it most, and its two users collaborate rather than compete — the exact thing that would sink a version aimed at teens.

Why this one, from me

This isn't a market abstraction for me. I've watched people I love work to hold onto their independence, and watched the rest of the family quietly carry the worry — and the tools that might have helped were never simple enough to actually get used. That gap, lived firsthand, is why I'd want to build this before anything else.

How I'd get there

I'd build it the way Buddy was built

Your own launch note said it: you surveyed hundreds of parents and co-built with 60 real families until it was right. Same method here — and I'd start from a place I already understand.

01

Listen where it's real

Talk to seniors aging in place and the adult kids coordinating their care — my own family included. Find the true shape of the worry before proposing a single pixel.

02

Prototype for simplicity

Put a stripped-down surface in front of real senior and adult-child pairs. Cut anything that isn't legible at arm's length. Protect dignity at every step.

03

Ship to the metric

Release into the warm Frame install base, watch daily senior engagement, and measure the worry we take off the family's plate.
